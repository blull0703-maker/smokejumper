Born from Fire

When California burns, the US Forest Service and CalFire clear thousands of acres of charred timber. SMOKEJUMPER wines begin there — with Fresno State students converting fire-scar slash into biochar, a carbon-rich soil amendment that turns scorched earth into fertile ground.

Cultivated by Students

Every vine in the SMOKEJUMPER program is tended by Fresno State viticulture students who amend vineyard soil with biochar, improving water retention and producing wines of uncommon depth and mineral complexity. This isn't a classroom exercise. It's a working winery.

Commemorated by Artists

Each vintage ships with a limited-edition medallion hand-carved from fire-affected wood by Fresno State art students. Real wood from real fires — a tactile record of the specific blaze that started the cycle.

The Cycle

01

Salvage

CalFire and US Forest Service crews clear fire scars across California. What most see as waste, we see as the starting line.

02

Enrich

Students process salvaged wood into biochar and work it into vineyard soil — locking carbon underground while building a living terroir.

03

Cultivate

Grapes develop slowly in biochar-amended earth, drawing mineral complexity from soil that remembers fire.

04

Craft

Small-lot wines are vinified by Fresno State enology students under professional mentorship — fermented with intention, aged with patience.

05

Savor

Each vintage debuts at an annual release gala that doubles as a fundraiser for The Holdfast, a planned climate resilience campus and museum of wildfire in Madera County.

06

Return

When vines reach the end of their productive life, students process the wood back into biochar. The cycle doesn't just repeat — it compounds.

The Science

Biochar-amended vineyard soils retained significantly more moisture than untreated plots during peak summer drought — with effects still measurable ten years after application.

— Baronti et al., Science of the Total Environment, 2022
10-year field trial, Tuscany

Across more than 280 controlled experiments worldwide, biochar improves plant water use efficiency by nearly 20%.

— Schmidt et al., GCB Bioenergy, 2021
Systematic review of 26 meta-analyses

A California Pinot Noir trial using biochar derived from Shasta County fire hazard forest residue produced a 34% yield increase over six harvests — with positive return on investment by the second harvest and quality unchanged.

— Hunt et al., Oasis Vineyard Field Trial, 2024
